Home Repairs You Can Do Yourself and Save Money

Published February 27, 2026 • 16 min read • by MonkeyRepair

The average homeowner spends $3,000 to $4,000 per year on home maintenance and repairs. A significant portion of that goes to labor costs for jobs that most people can handle themselves with basic tools and a YouTube tutorial. A plumber charges $150 to $300 just to show up, before any actual work starts. An electrician bills $75 to $150 per hour. A handyman charges $60 to $100 per hour.

Many common home repairs are simpler than you think. Fixing a running toilet takes 15 minutes and a $12 part. Patching a drywall hole costs $5 in materials and 30 minutes of your time. Replacing a light switch takes 10 minutes once you know how.

This guide covers the most common home repairs that homeowners can do themselves, with step-by-step instructions, the tools you need, and clear guidance on when a job is too complex or dangerous to DIY. Every repair listed here saves you $100 to $500 compared to hiring a professional.

1. Essential DIY Tool Kit

Before tackling any repair, make sure you have these basics. A well-stocked tool kit costs $100 to $200 upfront and pays for itself after your first avoided service call.

  • Cordless drill/driver. The most used tool in any homeowner's kit. Get a 20V model with multiple bits. $60 to $100.
  • Adjustable wrench set. For plumbing connections and nuts. $15 to $25 for a set of three.
  • Pliers (needle-nose and channel-lock). $10 to $20 each.
  • Screwdriver set. Phillips and flathead in multiple sizes. $15 to $25 for a complete set.
  • Tape measure. 25-foot is the standard. $10.
  • Level. A 24-inch level covers most jobs. $15.
  • Utility knife. For cutting drywall, caulk tubes, packaging. $8.
  • Stud finder. Essential for hanging anything heavy. $20 to $40.
  • Voltage tester. Non-contact type. Critical for any electrical work. $15 to $25.
  • Caulk gun. $8 to $12.
  • Putty knife set. For drywall compound and scraping. $10.
  • Plunger. Get a flange plunger for toilets and a cup plunger for sinks. $10 each.

2. Plumbing Repairs You Can Do Yourself

Fix a Running Toilet

Savings: $150 to $250 vs. calling a plumber.

A running toilet wastes up to 200 gallons of water per day and usually has one of three causes: a faulty flapper, a float set too high, or a leaking fill valve. Here is how to diagnose and fix each one:

  1. Remove the tank lid and look inside. Flush and watch the mechanism.
  2. Check the flapper. The rubber flapper at the bottom of the tank seals water in. If it is warped, cracked, or has mineral buildup, water leaks into the bowl. Turn off the water valve behind the toilet, flush to empty the tank, unhook the old flapper, and snap the new one onto the overflow tube ears. Universal flappers cost $5 to $8 at any hardware store.
  3. Check the float. If the water level is too high (above the overflow tube), the tank constantly drains. Adjust the float by turning the screw on the fill valve or bending the float arm down slightly. The water level should sit about 1 inch below the top of the overflow tube.
  4. Replace the fill valve. If the fill valve itself is leaking or running continuously, replace the entire unit. Turn off water, flush, remove the old valve (one nut under the tank), and install the new one. A Fluidmaster 400A universal fill valve costs $8 and comes with clear instructions.

Unclog a Drain Without Chemicals

Savings: $150 to $300 vs. calling a plumber.

Skip the chemical drain cleaners. They damage pipes over time and rarely clear serious clogs. Instead:

  1. Remove visible debris. Pull out the stopper or drain cover and remove hair or gunk by hand (wear gloves).
  2. Use a plunger. Fill the sink or tub with 2 to 3 inches of water. Place the plunger over the drain and pump vigorously 15 to 20 times. The pressure usually clears the clog.
  3. Use a drain snake. A 25-foot hand-crank drain snake ($15 to $25) reaches deeper clogs. Feed the cable into the drain, crank to push through the clog, then pull back. Run hot water to flush debris.
  4. Clean the P-trap. Place a bucket under the curved pipe beneath the sink. Unscrew the two slip nuts by hand, remove the P-trap, clean it out, and reinstall. This catches most sink clogs.

Fix a Dripping Faucet

Savings: $125 to $250 vs. calling a plumber.

A dripping faucet wastes up to 3,000 gallons per year. Most drips are caused by worn cartridges, O-rings, or washers. Turn off the water supply under the sink. Remove the faucet handle (usually a screw under a decorative cap). Pull out the cartridge or stem. Take the old parts to the hardware store and buy matching replacements. Reinstall in reverse order. Total parts cost: $5 to $20.

3. Electrical Repairs (Safe Ones)

Safety warning: Always turn off the breaker for the circuit you are working on and verify with a non-contact voltage tester before touching any wires. Electrical work can kill you if done improperly. These repairs are limited to simple, low-risk tasks.

Replace a Light Switch

Savings: $100 to $175 vs. calling an electrician.

  1. Turn off the breaker and confirm with a voltage tester that the switch is dead.
  2. Remove the switch plate cover (one or two screws).
  3. Unscrew the switch from the electrical box (two screws).
  4. Pull the switch out and note which wires connect to which terminals. Take a photo.
  5. Disconnect the wires (loosen the terminal screws or push the release slots).
  6. Connect the wires to the new switch in the same configuration. Hot (black) wire to the brass terminal, neutral (white) to the silver terminal, ground (bare copper or green) to the green terminal.
  7. Screw the switch into the box, replace the cover plate, and turn the breaker back on.

Replace a Light Fixture

Savings: $150 to $300 vs. calling an electrician.

Most light fixtures connect with just three wires: black to black (hot), white to white (neutral), and green or copper to green or copper (ground). Turn off the breaker. Remove the old fixture. Connect the matching wires with wire nuts. Mount the new fixture to the electrical box. Done.

Replace an Electrical Outlet

Savings: $100 to $150 vs. calling an electrician.

Same process as a light switch. Turn off breaker, verify with voltage tester, photograph the wire connections, disconnect old outlet, connect new outlet identically, and reassemble. Upgrade to a GFCI outlet in bathrooms, kitchens, and exterior locations for added safety.

4. Drywall Patching and Repair

Small Holes (Nail Holes to 1 Inch)

Savings: $100 to $200 vs. calling a handyman.

  1. Apply lightweight spackle directly into the hole with a putty knife.
  2. Smooth the surface flush with the wall.
  3. Let it dry (20 to 30 minutes for lightweight spackle).
  4. Sand lightly with 120-grit sandpaper.
  5. Apply a second thin coat if needed, sand again, and paint.

Medium Holes (1 to 6 Inches)

Savings: $150 to $300 vs. calling a handyman.

  1. Buy a drywall patch kit ($5 to $10) that includes a self-adhesive mesh patch.
  2. Clean loose debris from the hole.
  3. Apply the mesh patch over the hole, pressing firmly to stick.
  4. Cover the patch with joint compound using a wide putty knife (6 to 8 inches). Feather the edges outward for a smooth blend.
  5. Let dry completely (4 to 24 hours depending on compound type).
  6. Sand smooth with 120-grit, then 220-grit sandpaper.
  7. Apply a second coat of compound if the patch texture is still visible. Sand again.
  8. Prime and paint to match the surrounding wall.

Large Holes (6 Inches and Up)

Savings: $200 to $400 vs. calling a handyman.

  1. Cut a clean rectangle around the damaged area using a drywall saw.
  2. Cut a matching piece of new drywall.
  3. Install wood backing strips (1x3 lumber) behind the opening, secured with screws through the existing drywall.
  4. Screw the new drywall piece to the backing strips.
  5. Tape all seams with drywall tape (paper or mesh).
  6. Apply three thin coats of joint compound, sanding between each coat.
  7. Prime and paint.

5. Common Appliance Fixes

Dishwasher Not Draining

Savings: $150 to $250 vs. a service call.

Usually caused by a clogged filter or drain hose. Remove the bottom rack and pull out the filter assembly at the base of the tub. Clean all food debris. Check the drain hose where it connects to the garbage disposal or sink drain for kinks or clogs. Run a vinegar rinse cycle. This fixes the problem 80% of the time.

Refrigerator Not Cooling Properly

Savings: $100 to $200 vs. a service call.

Before calling for service, check these common causes: Dirty condenser coils (pull the fridge out and vacuum the coils on the back or bottom). Blocked air vents inside the fridge (rearrange items). Worn door gaskets (close the door on a dollar bill; if it slides out easily, the gasket needs replacing). Incorrect temperature settings (fresh food should be 37 degrees, freezer at 0 degrees).

Washing Machine Vibrating Excessively

Savings: $100 to $200 vs. a service call.

Check that the machine is level using a level placed on top. Adjust the leveling feet on the bottom until the bubble is centered. Ensure the load is balanced (redistributing clothes fixes most mid-cycle vibrations). Check that the shipping bolts were removed (common with new machines). Anti-vibration pads ($20 for a set of four) placed under each foot reduce noise and movement.

6. Doors and Windows

Fix a Sticking Door

Identify where the door sticks by looking for rub marks. If the top corner sticks, the top hinge may be loose. Tighten all hinge screws. If a screw spins freely (stripped hole), remove it, insert a wooden toothpick with wood glue into the hole, let it dry, and re-drive the screw. If the door edge rubs, use a hand plane or 80-grit sandpaper to remove material until it swings freely. Touch up with paint.

Replace Weatherstripping

Savings: $50 to $150 in energy costs per year.

Old, cracked weatherstripping around doors and windows lets in drafts that increase heating and cooling bills. Peel off the old weatherstripping. Clean the surface with rubbing alcohol. Apply new self-adhesive weatherstripping (V-strip or foam tape, $5 to $10 per door from any hardware store). Press firmly into place. This is a 15-minute job per door that pays for itself within months through reduced energy bills.

7. Interior Painting Like a Pro

Preparation Is Everything

Professional painters spend 70% of their time on prep and 30% painting. Amateurs do the opposite, which is why amateur paint jobs look amateur. Proper prep includes:

  • Move or cover all furniture. Lay drop cloths over floors.
  • Remove outlet and switch covers.
  • Fill all nail holes and cracks with spackle. Sand smooth after drying.
  • Clean walls with a damp cloth to remove dust and grease.
  • Apply painter's tape along ceilings, trim, and edges. Press the tape edge firmly to prevent bleed.
  • Prime any bare drywall, patches, or dark stains with a quality primer.

Painting Tips

  • Cut in edges with a 2.5-inch angled brush before rolling.
  • Use a 9-inch roller with the right nap for your wall texture (3/8 inch for smooth walls, 1/2 inch for light texture).
  • Load the roller evenly and apply in a W pattern, then fill in evenly.
  • Apply two coats minimum. Let the first coat dry completely before applying the second.
  • Remove painter's tape while the final coat is still slightly tacky for the cleanest lines.

8. Caulking and Sealing

Cracked or missing caulk around bathtubs, showers, sinks, and windows leads to water damage, mold, and energy loss. Re-caulking is a 30-minute job that prevents thousands of dollars in potential damage.

  1. Remove old caulk with a caulk removal tool or utility knife.
  2. Clean the surfaces with rubbing alcohol and let dry completely.
  3. Apply painter's tape on both sides of the joint for clean lines.
  4. Cut the caulk tube tip at a 45-degree angle. Start with a small opening; you can always cut more.
  5. Apply caulk in a steady, continuous bead along the joint.
  6. Smooth the bead with a wet finger or caulk smoothing tool.
  7. Remove the painter's tape immediately while the caulk is still wet.
  8. Let cure for 24 hours before exposing to water.

Use silicone caulk for bathrooms and kitchens (waterproof, mold-resistant). Use paintable acrylic caulk for trim and baseboards. Never use cheap caulk; a $6 tube of quality caulk outlasts a $2 tube by years.

9. When to Call a Professional

DIY has limits. These situations require licensed professionals:

  • Anything involving your main electrical panel. Breaker installation, panel upgrades, and heavy-gauge wiring are not DIY jobs.
  • Gas line work. Gas leaks can cause explosions. Never work on gas lines yourself.
  • Main sewer line clogs. If a snake does not clear it and multiple drains are backing up, the main line needs professional equipment.
  • Structural work. Removing or modifying load-bearing walls requires engineering assessment.
  • Roof repairs. Beyond replacing a few shingles, roof work requires safety equipment and experience.
  • HVAC repairs. Refrigerant handling and complex electrical components in AC and heating systems need licensed technicians.
  • Anything requiring permits. If your local building code requires a permit for the work, it usually needs a licensed contractor.

Frequently Asked Questions

What home repairs should you not do yourself?

Avoid DIY work on main electrical panels, gas lines, main sewer lines, structural modifications (load-bearing walls), major roof repairs, and HVAC systems. These require licensed professionals due to safety risks, code requirements, and the potential for expensive damage if done incorrectly.

What tools does every homeowner need?

A cordless drill, adjustable wrench set, pliers, screwdriver set, tape measure, level, utility knife, stud finder, non-contact voltage tester, caulk gun, putty knives, and a plunger. This basic kit costs $100 to $200 and handles 90% of common home repairs.

How much money can you save with DIY home repairs?

The average homeowner can save $1,000 to $3,000 per year by handling common repairs themselves. A single plumber visit costs $150 to $300, an electrician charges $75 to $150 per hour, and a handyman charges $60 to $100 per hour. Most repairs in this guide take under an hour with parts costing under $20.

How do I fix a running toilet?

The three most common causes are a faulty flapper ($5 to $8 replacement), a float set too high (adjust the screw or bend the arm), or a worn fill valve ($8 replacement). Turn off the water, flush to empty the tank, replace the faulty part, and turn the water back on. Total time: 15 to 30 minutes.

Is it safe to do electrical work yourself?

Simple tasks like replacing light switches, outlets, and light fixtures are safe for DIYers who follow proper precautions: always turn off the breaker, verify with a voltage tester, and follow the existing wiring configuration. Never work on the main panel, add new circuits, or work on live wires.

How do I patch a hole in drywall?

For small holes, apply spackle with a putty knife, let dry, and sand smooth. For medium holes (1-6 inches), use a self-adhesive mesh patch kit covered with joint compound. For large holes, cut a clean rectangle, install backing strips, screw in a new drywall piece, tape seams, and apply joint compound in three thin coats.

What is the easiest home repair to learn?

Fixing a running toilet is the easiest and most impactful first repair. It requires no special tools, the parts cost under $10, it takes 15 minutes, and it saves $150+ versus calling a plumber. Unclogging drains and replacing light switches are also excellent beginner repairs.

Where can I learn DIY home repair skills?

YouTube is the best free resource. Channels like This Old House, Home Mender, and See Jane Drill offer detailed tutorials for every repair level. Hardware stores like Home Depot and Lowe's also offer free in-store workshops and online how-to guides.

Start With One Repair

You do not need to become a master tradesperson. You just need to fix a running toilet, patch a hole in the wall, or replace a light switch one time to realize that most home repairs are well within your ability. Each repair you complete saves money and builds confidence for the next one.

Pick the repair from this guide that applies to your home right now. Watch a YouTube video for visual guidance. Buy the parts at your local hardware store. And fix it yourself. The first one is the hardest. After that, you will never call a handyman for the basics again.
