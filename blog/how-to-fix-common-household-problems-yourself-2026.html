How to Fix Common Household Problems Yourself in 2026

Published February 25, 2026 • 17 min read • by MonkeyRepair

The average homeowner spends over $3,000 per year on home repairs and maintenance. A huge chunk of that goes to paying someone else to fix things that you could easily handle yourself with basic tools and 20 minutes.

I am not talking about rewiring your electrical panel or replacing your roof. I am talking about the everyday stuff. The dripping faucet that is been driving you crazy for three months. The toilet that keeps running. The door that squeaks every time someone opens it at 2 AM.

This guide covers the most common household problems and shows you exactly how to fix each one yourself. No experience needed. No expensive tools. Just clear, simple instructions that anyone can follow.

1. How to Fix a Leaky Faucet

A dripping faucet wastes up to 3,000 gallons of water per year. That is money literally going down the drain. The fix usually takes 15 to 30 minutes.

What you need: Adjustable wrench, screwdriver, replacement washers or O-rings (bring the old ones to the hardware store to match)

Step by step:

  1. Turn off the water supply valves under the sink. Turn on the faucet to release remaining pressure.
  2. Remove the handle. Most handles have a decorative cap that pops off, revealing a screw underneath. Remove the screw and pull off the handle.
  3. Remove the packing nut with your wrench. Pull out the stem.
  4. Check the washer and O-ring at the bottom of the stem. If they are worn, cracked, or flat, that is your problem. Replace them.
  5. Reassemble everything in reverse order. Turn the water back on. No more drip.

For modern single-handle faucets, you might need to replace a cartridge instead. These are specific to your faucet brand. Look for the brand name on the faucet and search for the replacement cartridge. Most are under $15.

A basic plumbing repair kit from Amazon has all the washers, O-rings, and tools you need for most faucet repairs.

2. How to Fix a Running Toilet

A running toilet can waste 200 gallons of water per day. That is not a typo. Two hundred gallons. Per day. Fixing it yourself saves you hundreds on your water bill and takes about 20 minutes.

The three most common causes:

The Flapper

This is the rubber piece at the bottom of the tank that seals the flush valve. Over time it warps, gets mineral buildup, or just wears out. Lift the lid of your tank and look at it. If it is discolored, warped, or does not seal flat, replace it.

Turn off the water supply. Flush to empty the tank. Unhook the old flapper. Hook on the new one. Turn water back on. Done. Flappers cost about $5 at any hardware store.

The Fill Valve

If the flapper is fine, the fill valve might be the problem. This is the tall piece on the left side of the tank. If it keeps running or makes noise, it needs replacing.

Turn off water. Flush to empty. Disconnect the water supply line from the bottom. Remove the old fill valve and install the new one following the instructions in the box. A universal fill valve costs under $10 and fits most toilets.

The Float

The float tells the fill valve when to stop filling. If it is set too high, water rises above the overflow tube and runs constantly. Adjust the float height by turning the adjustment screw on the fill valve. Lower it until the water level sits about an inch below the top of the overflow tube.

3. How to Unclog a Drain

Before you call a plumber for a clogged drain, try these methods in order:

Method 1: Boiling water. Boil a full kettle of water. Pour it slowly down the drain in two or three stages, waiting a few seconds between each pour. This dissolves soap and grease buildup. Works surprisingly often for slow drains.

Method 2: Baking soda and vinegar. Pour half a cup of baking soda down the drain. Follow with half a cup of white vinegar. Cover the drain and wait 30 minutes. Flush with hot water. The chemical reaction breaks up organic clogs.

Method 3: Plunger. Use a cup plunger (the flat one, not the flanged one for toilets). Fill the sink with enough water to cover the plunger cup. Plunge vigorously 15 to 20 times. The pressure dislodges the clog.

Method 4: Drain snake. A drain snake from Amazon costs about $10 and physically breaks through clogs that other methods cannot handle. Insert it into the drain, turn the handle, and pull out the clog. Gross but effective.

What NOT to do: Avoid chemical drain cleaners like Drano. They work temporarily but damage your pipes over time, especially if you have older plumbing. The methods above are safer and more effective long-term.

4. How to Fix Squeaky Doors and Floors

Squeaky Door Hinges

This is one of the easiest fixes in your entire house. The squeak is caused by friction between the hinge pin and the knuckle. Here is all you do:

  1. Tap the hinge pin upward using a nail and hammer. Pull it out.
  2. Apply a thin coat of petroleum jelly, white lithium grease, or WD-40 to the pin.
  3. Slide the pin back in. Open and close the door a few times to distribute the lubricant.
  4. No more squeak.

Squeaky Hardwood Floors

Squeaky floors happen when floorboards rub against each other or against nails. The fix depends on whether you can access the floor from below:

From below (basement or crawl space): Have someone walk on the squeaky spot while you watch from below. Find the gap between the subfloor and joist. Shim a thin piece of wood into the gap with some wood glue. This stops the movement that causes the squeak.

From above: Sprinkle talcum powder or powdered graphite between the squeaky boards. Work it into the seams with a soft brush. This lubricates the boards and stops the friction. For a more permanent fix, drive a thin finish nail at an angle through the squeaky board into the subfloor below.

5. How to Patch Holes in Drywall

Whether it is from a doorknob, a misplaced nail, or that time you moved the couch, drywall holes happen. Here is how to fix them:

Small Holes (Nail or Screw Holes)

Fill with lightweight spackle using your finger or a small putty knife. Let it dry (about 30 minutes). Sand smooth with fine sandpaper. Touch up with paint. Done in 5 minutes of actual work.

Medium Holes (1 to 4 inches)

Use a self-adhesive drywall patch from the hardware store. Stick it over the hole. Apply a thin layer of joint compound over the patch. Let it dry. Apply a second thin coat. Let it dry. Sand smooth. Paint. Total cost: about $10 for the patch and compound.

Large Holes (Bigger than 4 inches)

Cut a piece of new drywall slightly larger than the hole. Trace around the new piece on the wall. Cut the wall along the traced line with a drywall saw. Install a backing board behind the opening. Screw the new drywall piece into the backing board. Tape the seams with drywall tape. Apply joint compound in thin coats, sanding between coats. Paint.

A drywall repair kit includes everything you need for small and medium patches.

6. How to Touch Up Paint Without Repainting a Room

The key to invisible touch-ups is using the exact same paint. This is why you should always keep leftover paint from every room. If you do not have the original can, take a chip of the paint to your hardware store and they can color-match it.

For small spots: Dip a small brush or foam roller in paint. Dab (do not brush) the paint onto the spot. Build up thin layers rather than one thick coat. Feather the edges to blend with the surrounding wall.

For larger areas: Use a roller that matches the original texture. Cut in the edges with a brush first. Roll from dry area into wet area to avoid visible lines. Apply thin coats.

Pro tip: Touch-ups are always more visible in direct light. If you cannot get a perfect match, paint from corner to corner on that wall. This gives you a natural break line and avoids visible patches.

7. How to Re-Caulk a Bathtub or Shower

Old, cracked, or moldy caulk is not just ugly. It lets water get behind your tub or shower, which can cause serious damage. Re-caulking takes about an hour and costs under $10.

  1. Remove old caulk. Use a caulk removal tool or a razor blade to scrape out all the old caulk. Be thorough. Get every bit of it.
  2. Clean the surface. Wipe down with rubbing alcohol or a bleach solution. Let it dry completely. Caulk does not stick to wet or dirty surfaces.
  3. Apply new caulk. Use 100% silicone caulk (not latex) for bathrooms. Cut the tip at a 45-degree angle. Apply a steady, continuous bead along the joint.
  4. Smooth it. Wet your finger and run it along the caulk bead in one smooth motion. This creates a clean, professional-looking seal.
  5. Let it cure. Do not use the tub or shower for 24 hours while the caulk cures.

8. How to Fix a Garbage Disposal

Before you panic, most garbage disposal problems have simple fixes:

It will not turn on at all: Press the reset button on the bottom of the unit. If that does not work, check the circuit breaker. These two fixes solve about 80 percent of "dead disposal" calls to plumbers.

It hums but will not spin: Something is jammed. NEVER put your hand inside a garbage disposal. Turn it off. Use the hex wrench that came with the disposal (or a quarter-inch Allen wrench) to manually rotate the blades from the bottom access point. This usually dislodges the jam. If you do not have a hex wrench, use a broom handle from the top to try to rotate the blades.

It leaks: Check three places. The sink flange (where the disposal connects to the sink), the dishwasher connection, and the discharge pipe. Tighten connections. Replace gaskets if needed.

9. How to Fix a Dead Electrical Outlet

Important safety note: Always turn off the circuit breaker before touching any electrical work. Use a voltage tester to confirm the power is off before you touch anything.

Most dead outlets have simple causes:

Check the GFCI. Your dead outlet might be controlled by a GFCI outlet elsewhere in the room. GFCI outlets have "Test" and "Reset" buttons. Press "Reset" on every GFCI outlet in the area. This often restores power to outlets downstream.

Check the circuit breaker. Go to your electrical panel and look for a tripped breaker. A tripped breaker sits in the middle position between on and off. Flip it all the way off, then back on.

Loose connections. If the breaker is fine and GFCI is not the issue, the outlet itself might have a loose wire. Turn off the breaker. Remove the outlet cover plate and the outlet from the box. Check that all wire connections are tight. Push the outlet back in and test.

If none of these work, call an electrician. Electrical problems beyond these basics can be dangerous.

10. How to Fix Drafty Windows and Doors

Drafts waste energy and money. Sealing them is cheap and easy:

For windows: Apply self-adhesive weatherstripping around the window frame where the sash meets the frame. For bigger gaps, use rope caulk (it is removable and does not damage paint). In winter, a window insulation film kit can reduce heat loss dramatically.

For doors: Install a door sweep at the bottom of exterior doors. Apply V-strip weatherstripping to the sides and top of the door frame. If you can see daylight around a closed door, that is where your heat (and money) is escaping.

For door frames: If there are gaps between the door frame and the wall, fill them with expanding foam insulation. Go slow with the foam because it expands a lot more than you expect.

11. The Essential DIY Toolkit

You do not need a garage full of tools to handle most home repairs. Here is what every homeowner should have:

  • Hammer - Get a 16-ounce claw hammer. It handles everything from hanging pictures to pulling nails.
  • Screwdriver set - Phillips and flathead in multiple sizes. Or get a multi-bit screwdriver that holds both.
  • Adjustable wrench - One 8-inch wrench handles most plumbing and general tightening needs.
  • Pliers - Needle-nose and channel-lock. Together they grip, twist, and pull just about anything.
  • Tape measure - A 25-foot tape measure covers any household measurement.
  • Level - For hanging anything straight. A 9-inch torpedo level works great.
  • Utility knife - Cuts drywall, opens boxes, trims caulk, and a hundred other things.
  • Drill/driver - A cordless drill saves enormous time on screws and small drilling jobs.
  • Flashlight - A good headlamp keeps your hands free when working in dark spaces.
  • Plunger - Get both a cup plunger (sinks) and a flange plunger (toilets).

A complete home tool kit from Amazon gets you started with everything above for under $50. It will pay for itself with your first DIY repair.

12. When to Actually Call a Professional

DIY saves money, but some jobs are genuinely dangerous or require licenses. Call a pro for:

  • Anything involving your main electrical panel - 200-amp panels can kill you.
  • Gas line work - Gas leaks cause explosions. Do not DIY anything involving gas lines.
  • Structural changes - Removing walls, adding beams, or anything that affects your home's structure needs an engineer and permits.
  • Roof work - Falls from roofs are one of the leading causes of home injury deaths.
  • Major plumbing - Replacing main sewer lines, water heaters, or anything requiring permits.
  • HVAC systems - Furnaces, air conditioners, and heat pumps require specialized knowledge and refrigerant handling licenses.
  • Anything you are not confident about - There is no shame in calling a professional. A bad DIY job often costs more to fix than having a pro do it right the first time.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the most common household problem to fix yourself?

A running or leaking toilet is the single most common household repair. It is also one of the easiest DIY fixes. Most running toilets just need a new flapper valve, which costs about $5 and takes 10 minutes to replace.

How much money can you save by doing home repairs yourself?

The average plumber charges $100 to $300 for a basic service call. An electrician charges $150 to $400. Most of the repairs in this guide can be done for under $20 in materials. If you DIY just four or five repairs per year instead of calling professionals, you save $500 to $1,500 annually.

What tools do I absolutely need as a homeowner?

At minimum: a hammer, screwdriver set, adjustable wrench, pliers, tape measure, utility knife, and a plunger. A cordless drill is the next thing to add. You can handle 90 percent of common household repairs with just these tools.

Is it safe to do electrical work yourself?

Simple tasks like replacing a light switch, outlet, or fixture are safe if you turn off the circuit breaker and verify the power is off with a voltage tester. Anything involving your main panel, adding new circuits, or working with 240-volt wiring should be done by a licensed electrician.

How do I know if a repair is too complicated for DIY?

If the repair requires permits in your area, involves gas lines, requires specialized equipment you do not own, or makes you feel unsafe, call a professional. A good rule of thumb: if a YouTube tutorial makes it look easy and you have the right tools, it is probably fine to DIY. If it looks complicated even in the tutorial, call a pro.

Where can I learn more DIY repair skills?

YouTube is the best free resource. Channels like This Old House, Home Mender, and See Jane Drill cover every repair imaginable. Your local hardware store (Home Depot, Lowe's) also offers free in-store workshops on basic home repair skills.
