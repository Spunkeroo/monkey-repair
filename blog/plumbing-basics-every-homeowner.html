Plumbing Basics Every Homeowner Should Know

The average American household spends $500-1,000 per year on plumbing repairs. The majority of those costs are labor for repairs that take a professional plumber 30-60 minutes. A running toilet, a dripping faucet, a slow drain. These are problems that cost $150-300 when you call a plumber and $5-20 when you fix them yourself. The difference is knowledge, and plumbing knowledge is more accessible than you think.

This guide covers the essential plumbing knowledge every homeowner needs. You will learn how your home's plumbing system works, how to handle common problems, when to call a professional, and how to maintain your system to prevent expensive failures. You do not need to become a plumber. You need to understand enough to handle the simple fixes and know when a problem is beyond your skill level.

How Your Home Plumbing Works

Every home plumbing system has two separate subsystems. The supply system brings fresh water into your home under pressure from the municipal water supply or a well pump. The drain-waste-vent (DWV) system carries used water and waste out of your home using gravity. Understanding this distinction helps you diagnose problems and communicate with plumbers when you need professional help.

The Supply System

Water enters your home through a main supply line, typically 3/4-inch or 1-inch pipe. It passes through a water meter (which measures consumption for billing) and a main shut-off valve (which stops all water flow to the house). From there, cold water branches to every fixture. A separate branch feeds the water heater, and hot water lines run from the heater to fixtures that need hot water. Supply lines are pressurized at 40-80 PSI.

The Drain-Waste-Vent System

Drain pipes carry water downhill by gravity from fixtures to the main sewer line or septic tank. Every drain has a P-trap, the U-shaped pipe under sinks that holds water to block sewer gases from entering your home. Vent pipes extend from drain lines up through the roof, allowing air into the system so water flows freely. Without vents, drains gurgle and drain slowly, similar to pouring water from an upside-down bottle versus one with a hole in the bottom.

Know Your Shut-Off Valves

The single most important plumbing knowledge is knowing where your shut-off valves are located. In an emergency such as a burst pipe or major leak, shutting off the water in seconds rather than minutes can save thousands of dollars in water damage.

Main Shut-Off Valve

Located where the main water supply enters your house. Common locations include the basement wall facing the street, near the water heater, in a utility closet, or outside near the water meter. Find it now, before an emergency. Test it by turning it clockwise to close, then open a faucet to confirm water stops. If the valve is stiff or does not fully close, have it replaced. A failed shut-off valve during a pipe burst is a nightmare.

Individual Fixture Shut-Offs

Most sinks and toilets have individual shut-off valves. Under sinks, you will find two valves on the wall: one for hot and one for cold. Behind toilets, there is usually one valve on the wall or floor. These allow you to shut off water to a single fixture for repairs without affecting the rest of the house.

Emergency Action Plan

If a pipe bursts: immediately shut off the main water valve. Then open a faucet at the lowest point in your home to drain remaining water from the pipes. Call a plumber. While waiting, use towels, buckets, and a wet-dry vacuum to manage water. Document damage with photos for insurance.

Fixing Common Toilet Problems

Running Toilet (Most Common)

A running toilet wastes up to 200 gallons per day and can cost $50-100 per month in water bills. The fix is almost always a worn flapper valve. Remove the tank lid, flush the toilet, and watch the flapper at the bottom of the tank. If it does not seal completely, water leaks from the tank to the bowl, and the fill valve runs continuously to compensate.

The fix takes five minutes: turn off the water supply behind the toilet, flush to drain the tank, unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube, and snap on a new universal flapper ($5-8 at any hardware store). Turn the water back on. Problem solved.

Toilet Running Intermittently (Phantom Flush)

The toilet fills randomly without being flushed. Same cause as above: a slowly leaking flapper. The tank gradually loses water until the fill valve activates to refill it. Replace the flapper.

Weak Flush

Usually caused by clogged rim jets under the toilet bowl rim. Mineral deposits block the small holes that direct water into the bowl during flushing. Clean them with a wire hanger or small brush. For persistent weak flushing, the fill valve may not be allowing the tank to fill completely. Adjust the float height so the water level reaches about one inch below the overflow tube.

Toilet Will Not Stop Filling

If the water level rises above the overflow tube and continuously drains, the fill valve is not shutting off. Adjust the float height first. If that does not work, the fill valve needs replacement. A universal fill valve costs $8-15 and takes 15-20 minutes to install with no special tools.

Unclogging Drains the Right Way

The Plunger (Try This First)

A good plunger solves most drain clogs. For sinks, use a cup plunger (flat bottom). For toilets, use a flange plunger (the one with the extended rubber lip). Fill the sink or toilet with enough water to cover the plunger cup. Plunge vigorously 15-20 times, maintaining the seal. The pressure wave dislodges most clogs.

Drain Snake (For Stubborn Clogs)

A hand-crank drain snake ($20-30) reaches 25-50 feet into pipes to break through or retrieve clogs that a plunger cannot clear. Feed the cable into the drain while cranking the handle. When you feel resistance, you have reached the clog. Continue cranking to break through or hook the debris. Pull it out slowly.

Avoid Chemical Drain Cleaners

Products like Drano and Liquid-Plumr work on minor clogs but damage your pipes with repeated use. The caustic chemicals can corrode metal pipes, soften PVC joints, and generate heat that weakens pipe integrity. They are also dangerous if they splash. A plunger and drain snake are more effective, cheaper, and do not damage your plumbing.

Fixing Leaky Faucets

A dripping faucet wastes 3,000-5,000 gallons of water per year and adds $20-50 annually to your water bill. The fix depends on the faucet type:

  • Compression faucets (two handles): Replace the rubber washer or seat. Turn off water, remove the handle, unscrew the packing nut, remove the stem, and replace the worn washer at the bottom.
  • Cartridge faucets (single handle, Moen style): Replace the cartridge. Moen provides free replacement cartridges for life for many models.
  • Ceramic disc faucets (single handle, Delta style): Clean or replace the disc cylinder. These are the most durable and rarely need repair.

Before attempting any faucet repair, identify your faucet brand and model. Manufacturers have specific replacement parts, and using the correct part ensures a proper fix. Search the brand name plus the model number for specific repair instructions.

Water Heater Maintenance

Annual Flush

Sediment accumulates at the bottom of your water heater tank, reducing efficiency and shortening its lifespan. Annual flushing takes 30 minutes and costs nothing. Turn off the heater (gas: set to pilot; electric: turn off breaker). Attach a garden hose to the drain valve at the bottom. Run the hose to a floor drain or outside. Open the drain valve and let water flow until it runs clear. Close the valve, remove the hose, and restart the heater.

Temperature Setting

The Department of Energy recommends 120 degrees Fahrenheit. This is hot enough for comfortable showers and dishwashing while reducing scalding risk and saving energy. Every 10 degrees you lower the temperature saves 3-5% on water heating costs.

Anode Rod Inspection

The anode rod is a sacrificial metal rod that corrodes instead of your tank. When it is depleted, the tank starts corroding. Check it every 2-3 years by unscrewing it from the top of the tank. If more than half of the rod is corroded or it is less than half an inch thick, replace it ($20-50 for the rod). This single maintenance item can double your water heater's lifespan.

Preventing Plumbing Emergencies

  • Never pour grease down drains. Let it solidify in a container and throw it in the trash. Grease is the number one cause of drain clogs.
  • Replace washing machine hoses every 5 years. Burst washing machine hoses are one of the most common causes of home water damage. Replace rubber hoses with braided stainless steel hoses for extra durability.
  • Insulate exposed pipes in winter. Frozen pipes burst and cause catastrophic water damage. Insulate pipes in unheated areas (garage, crawl space, attic) with foam pipe insulation.
  • Do not ignore slow drains. A slow drain is a partial clog that will become a full clog. Address it early with a plunger or snake before it becomes an emergency.
  • Know your water pressure. High water pressure (above 80 PSI) stresses pipes, fittings, and appliances. Install a pressure gauge on a hose bib to check. If pressure is too high, install a pressure reducing valve.

When to Call a Plumber

  • No water in the house (main line issue)
  • Sewer line backup (multiple drains backing up simultaneously)
  • Water heater leaking from the tank itself (not connections)
  • Gas water heater pilot light issues or gas smell
  • Frozen or burst pipes inside walls
  • Any work involving the main sewer line
  • Low water pressure throughout the house
  • Slab leaks (water coming up through the foundation)
  • Any situation where you are unsure and the potential for water damage is high

Frequently Asked Questions

What plumbing can a homeowner do themselves?

Homeowners can replace toilet flappers and fill valves, unclog drains, replace faucets and showerheads, fix running toilets, replace wax rings, and make basic pipe repairs with push-fit connectors. These cover the majority of common plumbing issues.

How do you shut off the water in an emergency?

Find your main shut-off valve where water enters your home (basement, crawl space, or near the meter). Turn clockwise to close. Individual fixtures have shut-off valves under sinks and behind toilets for localized issues. Know these locations before an emergency.

How often should you drain your water heater?

Drain and flush annually to remove sediment that reduces efficiency and shortens lifespan. Takes 30 minutes: turn off heater, attach hose to drain valve, flush until water runs clear. This simple task can extend your water heater's life by years.

Why does my toilet keep running?

Almost always a worn flapper valve. The rubber flapper at the tank bottom does not seal, so water leaks continuously into the bowl. Replace it with a universal flapper ($5-8) in five minutes. A running toilet wastes up to 200 gallons per day.

What should I never put down a drain?

Never put cooking grease/oil, coffee grounds, eggshells, flour/starch, fibrous vegetables, paint, medications, or chemical drain cleaners down any drain. These cause clogs, pipe damage, and environmental harm. Grease is the number one cause of household drain clogs.
