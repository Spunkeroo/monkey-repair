Plumbing Repairs

Fix a Running Toilet (Save $140-280)

A running toilet is the single most common plumbing problem, and it is almost always caused by one of two cheap parts: the flapper or the fill valve. Before calling a plumber, try these fixes:

Flapper replacement (5 minutes, $5-8):

  1. Turn off the water supply valve behind the toilet.
  2. Flush to empty the tank.
  3. Unhook the old flapper from the overflow tube ears and disconnect the chain from the flush lever.
  4. Take the old flapper to the hardware store to match the size, or buy a universal flapper like the Korky 100BP.
  5. Hook the new flapper onto the overflow tube ears and connect the chain to the flush lever with about half an inch of slack.
  6. Turn the water back on and test.

Fill valve replacement (20 minutes, $8-15):

  1. Turn off the water supply and flush the tank completely. Sponge out remaining water.
  2. Disconnect the water supply line from the bottom of the tank.
  3. Unscrew the locknut holding the old fill valve from underneath the tank.
  4. Remove the old fill valve and install the new one (Fluidmaster 400A is the industry standard). Adjust the height to match your tank.
  5. Hand-tighten the locknut, reconnect the supply line, and turn the water on.
  6. Adjust the water level to about one inch below the top of the overflow tube.

Unclog a Drain (Save $145-325)

Most drain clogs can be cleared without a plumber. Try these methods in order:

  1. Boiling water: Pour a full kettle of boiling water directly down the drain. This dissolves soap scum and grease buildup. Works for minor clogs.
  2. Plunger: Use a cup plunger for sinks and a flange plunger for toilets. Fill the sink with enough water to cover the plunger cup, then plunge vigorously 15-20 times. The pressure breaks up the clog.
  3. Drain snake: A 25-foot drain snake ($15-25 at any hardware store) handles most clogs that a plunger cannot. Feed the snake into the drain, turn the handle when you hit resistance, and pull back. Most bathroom clogs are hair buildup 12-18 inches into the pipe.
  4. Baking soda and vinegar: Pour half a cup of baking soda down the drain, followed by half a cup of white vinegar. Cover the drain and wait 30 minutes. Flush with hot water. This works for mild organic clogs.
Avoid Chemical Drain Cleaners

Products like Drano and Liquid-Plumr contain sodium hydroxide (lye) or sulfuric acid that can corrode pipes, especially older metal pipes. They are also dangerous if they splash back during use. A $15 drain snake is safer, more effective, and reusable for years.

Replace a Kitchen Faucet (Save $200-300)

Replacing a kitchen faucet looks intimidating but is straightforward. Most faucets come with everything you need and detailed instructions. The entire job takes 30-60 minutes.

  1. Turn off hot and cold water supply valves under the sink.
  2. Disconnect the supply lines from the old faucet using an adjustable wrench.
  3. Remove the mounting nuts holding the old faucet. A basin wrench ($15) makes this much easier in tight spaces.
  4. Clean the sink surface where the old faucet sat.
  5. Install the new faucet following the manufacturer's instructions. Most use a single mounting nut with a rubber gasket.
  6. Connect the supply lines (use braided stainless steel lines for durability).
  7. Turn on the water and check for leaks. Tighten connections if needed.

Drywall and Painting

Patch a Drywall Hole (Save $185-470)

Drywall damage from doorknobs, furniture, and accidental impacts is incredibly common. The repair is simple regardless of hole size.

Small holes (under 3 inches):

  1. Buy a self-adhesive mesh drywall patch ($3-5 at any hardware store).
  2. Stick it over the hole.
  3. Apply joint compound (spackle) over the patch with a putty knife. Spread it thin and slightly beyond the edges of the patch.
  4. Let it dry completely (2-4 hours or per label instructions).
  5. Sand smooth with 120-grit sandpaper.
  6. Apply a second thin coat if needed, sand again, and paint.

Large holes (3-6 inches):

  1. Cut a piece of new drywall slightly larger than the hole.
  2. Hold the patch over the hole and trace around it with a pencil.
  3. Cut along the traced line using a drywall saw to create a clean, square opening.
  4. Cut a piece of plywood or a drywall backer board slightly larger than the opening. Insert it behind the drywall and screw through the existing drywall into the backer to hold it in place.
  5. Screw the drywall patch to the backer board.
  6. Apply mesh tape over the seams, then apply two coats of joint compound, sanding between coats.
  7. Prime and paint to match the wall.

Paint a Room Like a Professional (Save $350-700)

Professional painters charge $400-800 per room, but materials cost only $50-100. The key to a professional finish is preparation:

  • Fill all nail holes and cracks with spackle. Sand smooth when dry.
  • Clean walls with a damp cloth to remove dust and grease.
  • Use painter's tape (FrogTape or ScotchBlue) on trim, ceiling lines, and around outlets.
  • Use a primer if you are covering dark colors, stains, or new drywall patches.
  • Cut in edges with a 2.5-inch angled brush first, then roll the large areas with a 9-inch roller.
  • Use a W or M pattern when rolling to distribute paint evenly.
  • Apply two coats, allowing full dry time between coats (2-4 hours for latex paint).
  • Remove tape while the final coat is still slightly tacky for the cleanest lines.

Electrical Fixes

Replace a Light Switch or Outlet (Save $97-240)

Replacing a standard light switch or outlet is one of the simplest electrical jobs. Always follow safety precautions:

  1. Turn off the circuit breaker for the circuit you are working on. Test the switch or outlet with a voltage tester to confirm power is off. Never skip this step.
  2. Remove the cover plate and unscrew the device from the electrical box.
  3. Take a photo of the wiring before disconnecting anything so you can replicate it on the new device.
  4. Disconnect the wires from the old device and connect them to the new one in the same configuration.
  5. Screw the new device into the box, install the cover plate, and turn the breaker back on.

Replace a Light Fixture (Save $120-300)

Swapping a light fixture is similar to replacing a switch but involves working overhead. The key steps are:

  1. Turn off the circuit breaker and verify with a voltage tester.
  2. Remove the old fixture by unscrewing it from the ceiling box and disconnecting the wires.
  3. Install the mounting bracket that comes with the new fixture.
  4. Connect the wires: black to black (hot), white to white (neutral), and bare copper to green (ground).
  5. Secure connections with wire nuts and tuck wires into the box.
  6. Mount the new fixture to the bracket, install bulbs, and turn the breaker on.

Flooring Repairs

Fix Squeaky Hardwood Floors (Save $190-470)

Squeaky floors are caused by wood rubbing against nails or subfloor. Two reliable fixes:

  • From above: Drive trim-head screws through the hardwood into the subfloor/joist at the squeak location. Countersink the screw and fill the hole with wood filler that matches your floor.
  • From below (if accessible): Have someone walk on the squeak while you mark its location from the basement. Drive screws up through the subfloor into the hardwood, being careful not to penetrate the floor surface. Use short screws (1.25 inches) to avoid poking through.

Repair Scratched Hardwood (Save $100-300)

  • Light scratches: Apply matching wood stain with a fine brush or cotton swab. Wipe away excess and apply polyurethane over the area.
  • Deep scratches: Fill with wood filler matched to your floor color. Sand smooth when dry, apply stain, then polyurethane.
  • Prevention: Put felt pads on all furniture legs, keep pet nails trimmed, and use area rugs in high-traffic areas.

Appliance Repairs

Fix a Dishwasher Not Draining (Save $150-350)

Before calling a repair service, check these common causes:

  1. Clean the filter: Most modern dishwashers have a removable filter at the bottom of the tub. Twist and pull it out, clean off food debris, and reinstall. This fixes the problem 50% of the time.
  2. Check the drain hose: Look under the sink where the dishwasher drain hose connects. It should have a high loop (as high as possible under the counter) to prevent backflow. If it is kinked or blocked, straighten or clear it.
  3. Clean the air gap: If your sink has an air gap (a small dome-shaped device next to the faucet), remove the cap and clean any debris inside.
  4. Run the garbage disposal: The dishwasher often drains into the disposal. If the disposal is clogged, the dishwasher cannot drain. Run the disposal with water flowing to clear it.

Fix a Refrigerator Not Cooling (Save $200-500)

  1. Check condenser coils: Dusty coils are the number one cause of refrigerator cooling problems. Pull the fridge out from the wall and vacuum the coils on the back or bottom. Do this every 6-12 months for optimal efficiency.
  2. Check the door seal: Close the door on a dollar bill. If you can pull it out easily, the seal is not tight enough. Clean the gasket with warm soapy water. If it is cracked or warped, replace it ($20-50 from the manufacturer).
  3. Check the evaporator fan: If you hear a clicking or squealing noise, the evaporator fan (inside the freezer behind the back panel) may need replacement. This is a $20-40 part that takes 20 minutes to swap.
  4. Verify temperature settings: The refrigerator should be at 37-40 degrees F and the freezer at 0 degrees F. Adjust if someone accidentally changed the settings.

Exterior Maintenance

Seal Cracks in Driveway or Patio (Save $200-600)

Concrete and asphalt crack over time due to temperature changes and ground movement. Sealing cracks prevents water from penetrating and causing further damage (especially in freeze-thaw climates).

  1. Clean cracks thoroughly with a wire brush and blow out loose debris.
  2. For cracks under 0.5 inches: use concrete crack filler ($5-10). Apply with a caulking gun and smooth with a putty knife.
  3. For larger cracks: use self-leveling polyurethane sealant ($8-15). Pour directly into the crack and it levels itself.
  4. For asphalt: use rubberized asphalt crack filler ($8-12). Apply and smooth before it skins over.

Clean and Maintain Gutters (Save $150-350)

Clogged gutters cause water damage to fascia, soffits, foundations, and landscaping. Clean them at least twice a year, in spring and fall.

  • Use a sturdy ladder with a stabilizer bar. Never lean the ladder directly against gutters.
  • Scoop debris by hand (wear gloves) or use a gutter scoop tool.
  • Flush gutters with a garden hose to check for proper flow and identify leaks.
  • Repair leaking joints with gutter sealant ($5).
  • Consider installing gutter guards ($1-10 per linear foot) to reduce future cleaning.

HVAC Maintenance

Replace HVAC Filters (Save $100-200/year in energy)

A dirty HVAC filter forces your system to work harder, increasing energy bills by 5-15% and shortening equipment life. Replace filters every 1-3 months depending on usage, pets, and air quality.

  • Locate your filter (usually in the return air duct or air handler unit).
  • Note the size printed on the existing filter (e.g., 20x25x1).
  • Buy the correct size at any hardware store. MERV 8-11 filters offer good filtration without restricting airflow.
  • Install with the airflow arrow pointing toward the ductwork (toward the furnace/air handler).
  • Write the installation date on the filter edge with a marker so you know when to replace it next.

When to Call a Professional

While DIY saves money, some jobs require professional expertise for safety, legal, or quality reasons. Always call a professional for:

  • Electrical panel work: Main panel upgrades, new circuit installations, and anything involving the service entrance require a licensed electrician and permits.
  • Gas line work: Gas leaks can cause explosions. Any gas line repair, installation, or modification must be done by a licensed professional.
  • Structural modifications: Removing or modifying load-bearing walls, foundation repairs, and structural reinforcement require engineering assessment and professional contractors.
  • Roof replacement: While minor repairs (replacing a few shingles) are DIY-able, full roof replacement involves safety risks and warranty considerations that justify professional work.
  • Asbestos and mold: If your home was built before 1980, disturbing certain materials could release asbestos fibers. Professional testing and remediation are required by law in many jurisdictions.
  • Plumbing beyond fixtures: Main sewer line repairs, water heater installation, and work requiring permits should be done by licensed plumbers.

Frequently Asked Questions

What home repairs should you not do yourself?

Avoid DIY work on main electrical panel upgrades, gas line repairs, structural modifications, full roof replacement, asbestos or mold remediation, and any work requiring permits that specify licensed contractors. These jobs carry serious safety risks, legal requirements, or warranty implications that make professional help essential.

How much money can you save doing home repairs yourself?

The average homeowner can save $3,000 to $10,000 per year by handling common repairs themselves. Labor typically accounts for 50-70% of a contractor's bill. Simple jobs like fixing a running toilet ($150-300 plumber cost vs $15 DIY), patching drywall ($200-500 vs $30), or replacing a faucet ($250-450 vs $50) add up quickly over the course of a year.

What are the easiest home repairs for beginners?

The easiest home repairs for beginners include replacing toilet flappers and fill valves, patching small drywall holes, caulking around tubs and windows, replacing light switches and outlets, fixing squeaky doors with WD-40 or hinge pin replacement, replacing cabinet hardware, and cleaning or replacing HVAC filters. All of these can be learned in under an hour.

What tools do I need for basic home repairs?

A basic home repair toolkit should include a cordless drill/driver, hammer, tape measure, level, adjustable wrench, pliers (needle-nose and channel-lock), screwdriver set (Phillips and flathead), utility knife, putty knife, stud finder, flashlight, and a basic socket set. Budget about $150-250 for a quality starter kit that covers 90% of common household repairs.

Is it worth learning home repair skills?

Absolutely. Learning basic home repair skills saves thousands of dollars annually, allows you to handle emergencies immediately rather than waiting days for a contractor, increases your home's value through proper maintenance, and gives you confidence as a homeowner. Most basic repairs can be learned in under an hour through YouTube tutorials, manufacturer guides, and articles like this one.
